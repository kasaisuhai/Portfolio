SLAM Redesign

Salt Lake Academy of Music is a non-profit based in Utah that provides after-school lessons, performance, composition, songwriting, recording, and summer camp programs for K-12 kids of all backgrounds, and skill levels. When looking at their website, I knew that it needed to be redesigned. It was very cluttered and finding information about things like classes and instructors was very difficult. During the process of redesigning the website, my team and I made it much cleaner and made it responsive.

After

Research

Starting out, we needed to figure out who we were designing for, so we made a proto-persona in order to better understand who the user would be. It was obvious that it would either be a kid who is interested in learning music, or a parent of said kid. We decided to focus on the parent for this redesign.

The next step was conducting user interviews, selecting for parents of children who were interested in music. Our questions focused on what they would look for in a music school, and what was most important for their children's musical education. After the interviews, we organized the results with an affinity map in order to better understand our findings.

We also did a heuristic evaluation on the original website. There was a lot of clutter, and some of the navigation items went to the same page. We knew that the SLAM website was cluttered but by doing the heuristic evaluation we were able to figure out just how bad it was.

Definition & Ideation

From our work so far, we found that SLAM's website is busy, cluttered, and inconsistent, which makes it difficult for parents interested in enrolling their children to find information and make a decision. We hypothesized that by cutting out inconsistencies and condensing the layout of the site to mainly present information that's relevant to these users, it would make the site easier to read and navigate, therefore making it easier for parents to find the information they need.

As for the navigation, we knew that we needed to create a new site map. It allowed us to organize and structure the content and features of the website in a logical and intuitive manner. We carefully considered Audrey's motivations, ensuring that the site map aligned with her goals of finding suitable music programs for her children and understanding the advantages of choosing SLAM. This allowed us to simplify and declutter the navigation.

Prototyping

Before designing a prototype, we created a style guide that we could base our visuals off of. Overall we kept most of the colors that Slam already had because we wanted to create consistency and cohesion in the brand's visual identity across the website. The original website used quite a few different fonts, so we chose to use Nunito Sans for headers and Open Sans for body text in order to keep text consistent.

Moving on, we created a mid-fi prototype, which served as an essential tool for validating our design decisions, incorporating user feedback, and fine-tuning the user experience.

Testing

Once we had finished our mid-fi prototype, we could move on to testing. The tasks that we tested were filling out and submitting a contact form, and viewing instructors. From the tests we realized that by putting instructors in the About page, it was more difficult for users to find. We decided that it would be best to move the instructors to the lessons page because most of the testers went to that page when looking for them.

Conclusion

Through this experience, I reinstilled the importance of having a good website design that not only attracts users but also instills confidence in your product. A well-designed website can make all the difference in creating a positive user experience and building trust with your audience.
